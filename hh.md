## A Deeper Dive
#### Quick Facts:
- Safe long‑term listening: **≤ 85 dB**
- Every +3 dB halves safe exposure time  
- Distance matters: doubling distance ≈ **–6 dB**
- Temporary ringing = your ears asking for a break  
- Hearing damage is cumulative and permanent  

We only get one pair of ears, and they’re far more sensitive than most people realize. They let us enjoy everything from the softest details in a quiet room to the full-body impact of a live show. But like any finely tuned system, they have limits. Push past those limits often enough, and the damage becomes permanent.

This page is meant to be a practical, honest guide to understanding how loud sound affects hearing, why distance and time matter so much, and how to enjoy this speaker responsibly without causing unjust fear.


## Why Loud Sound Can Be Harmful

Inside your inner ear are tiny hair cells that convert vibrations into electrical signals your brain interprets as sound. They’re delicate, and they don’t regenerate. Once they’re damaged, they’re gone for good.

Two factors determine how much stress you put on them:

- **How loud the sound is**
- **How long you’re exposed to it**

A short burst of something extremely loud can be just as harmful as a long session of something moderately loud. Your ears don’t give you real‑time warnings. 
**There is no “pain threshold” that reliably tells you when you’re crossing into danger.**


## Distance: The Safety Factor
Sound intensity drops off fast with distance. If you double your distance from a sound source, the level drops by roughly **6 dB**, which is a huge difference.

This is why:

- Standing right in front of a this speaker can hit 110–120 dB  
- The back of the room might be 95–100 dB  

In a small room, especially in nearfield setups, it’s easy to reach “concert loud” without realizing how little distance your ears have from the speaker.


## The Time Factor: How Long Is Too Long?

The relationship between loudness and safe exposure time isn’t linear, it’s exponential. Every **3 dB increase halves the safe listening duration**.

A simple reference:

- **85 dB** → ~8 hours  
- **88 dB** → ~4 hours  
- **91 dB** → ~2 hours  
- **94 dB** → ~1 hour  
- **97 dB** → ~30 minutes  
- **100 dB** → ~15 minutes  
- **110 dB** → ~55 seconds
- **120 dB** → ~3 seconds 



## What “Dangerous” Actually Feels Like

One of the tricky things about hearing is that **loud sound often feels good**. It’s immersive, energetic, and exciting. Your ears don’t warn you in the moment.

The warning signs show up *after* the exposure:

- Ringing or buzzing (tinnitus)  
- Muffled hearing  
- A sense of fullness or pressure  
- Needing to turn up the volume more than usual  

These are signs of **temporary threshold shift (TTS)**. It's your ears’ way of saying they were pushed too far. It often fades, but it’s also a sign that damage is accumulating. Incur this damage too many times, or too severely, and the TTS will never fade.

At the highest levels that this speaker can produce, at 1m, **TTS occurs instantly and without warning**.
